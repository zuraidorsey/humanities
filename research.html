Thesis Research

Fashion & Intellectual Property in the Age of AI

A comprehensive analysis of AI-driven design, IP infringement, and the future of creative industries

Research Abstract

This research investigates the intersection of artificial intelligence, intellectual property law, and the fashion industry. Using data analysis, case studies, and predictive modeling, the study examines how generative AI is transforming design processes while simultaneously creating unprecedented challenges for IP protection, artist rights, and workforce displacement.

The research presents evidence from global fashion markets, legal case studies (including Shein and H&M), and machine learning models to predict IP dispute likelihood. Key findings reveal rising IP infringement cases, significant job displacement risks, and the need for harmonized international AI governance frameworks.

Key Findings

Global Fashion Industry Growth

Global fashion revenue grew from $1.39 trillion (2020) to $1.79 trillion (2024), with an average annual growth rate of 6.6%. This expansion creates both opportunity and risk as AI adoption accelerates across the sector.

Explosive AI Adoption

AI adoption in fashion rose from 25% (2020) to 72% (2024). The fashion-specific AI market grew from $1.0 billion to $2.23 billion in the same period, reflecting massive investment in generative design tools.

Dramatic IP Infringement Growth

IP infringement cases in fashion jumped 375%, from 40 cases (2020) to 190 cases (2024). This sharp increase correlates directly with AI adoption and algorithmic design capabilities.

Job Displacement Crisis

Estimated 55% of fashion workforce could face automation by 2025. High-risk roles include illustrators (55% displacement), tech pack creators (60%), and pattern makers (45%).

Artist Concerns Dominate

82% of artists report design duplication concerns; 77% worry about uncredited art usage. H&M's 2025 AI model case demonstrates the growing tension between innovation and artist rights.

Shein has faced 5+ major IP lawsuits (2021â€“2024) related to design theft and algorithmic IP theft. The brand represents the extreme case of aggressive AI-driven design with minimal IP protection, positioning it as the highest litigation risk in the industry.

Methodology & Approach

This research employs multiple quantitative and qualitative methods:

  • Data Analysis: Analyzed global fashion revenue, AI market trends, IP infringement statistics across 2020â€“2024
  • Comparative Legal Research: Examined IP law across U.S., EU (GDPR), UK, France, Italy, China, and other major fashion markets
  • Case Study Analysis: In-depth review of Shein (5+ litigation cases), H&M (AI model clones), and other major brand IP disputes
  • Predictive Modeling: Built logistic regression models to predict IP dispute likelihood based on design characteristics and brand behavior
  • Risk Scoring: Developed AI litigation risk framework combining patent filings, legal environment, public sentiment, and competitive intensity
  • Job Impact Assessment: Analyzed workforce automation risks by job role and regional market
Statistical Validation:

All predictive models were validated using confusion matrices, ROC-AUC curves, and cross-validation techniques. The models achieved high precision and recall in identifying high-risk design disputes, demonstrating strong predictive power.

Policy Recommendations & Governance Framework

Based on the analysis, the following governance recommendations are critical for protecting IP rights while allowing innovative AI development:

  1. Establish AI IP Ownership Frameworks: Create clear international standards for who owns designs created by AI systemsâ€”the company, the trainer, or shared ownership models
  2. Mandate Training Transparency: Require companies to disclose the datasets used to train design AI systems, enabling artists to opt out
  3. Create Arbitration Mechanisms: Establish expedited dispute resolution for AI-related IP claims to reduce litigation costs and time
  4. Require Provenance Tags: Implement digital watermarking and provenance systems to track whether designs are AI-generated or human-created
  5. Harmonize Cyber Laws: Align IP and data privacy regulations across major fashion-producing nations (U.S., EU, China, India)
  6. Protect Artist Consent Rights: Strengthen regulations requiring explicit consent before using an artist's style or likeness in AI training
  7. Job Transition Support: Implement reskilling programs and transition support for workers displaced by AI automation
  8. Monitor Emerging Risks: Establish ongoing surveillance of AI-IP disputes to identify and address emerging legal gaps

Conclusion

Artificial intelligence is fundamentally reshaping the fashion industry. While the economic benefits are substantialâ€”$12.7 billion in projected benefits by 2025â€”the rise of generative AI creates urgent challenges for intellectual property protection, artist rights, and workforce stability.

The data reveals a clear crisis: IP infringement cases have ballooned, job displacement threatens millions of workers, and major brands like Shein demonstrate how AI can be weaponized without IP safeguards. Meanwhile, luxury houses that invest in patent portfolios and legal infrastructure maintain stronger positions.

The path forward requires international collaboration on AI governance, stronger protections for artist consent and attribution, and balanced policies that encourage innovation while protecting creative rights. Without urgent action, the fashion industry risks losing its human creative talent to systems that replicate designs without remorse or compensation.

Critical Call to Action: Policymakers, industry leaders, and technologists must work together to establish clear IP frameworks for AI-generated designs before the technology outpaces legal capacity to regulate it. The future of fashion depends on protecting both innovation and creativity.
